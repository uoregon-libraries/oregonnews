Morning democrat
[LCCN: sn 85042398]
Baker City, Baker County, Oregon
1892 – 1929

As a social hub on the historic Oregon Trail, Baker City, established in 1874, served as a support center for early gold mining in Eastern Oregon. Inevitably, Baker City’s early newspapers featured coverage of local mining events and opportunities.

The Baker City Morning Democrat [LCCN: sn85042398] grew out of Baker’s City’s first newspaper, the Bedrock Democrat [LCCN: sn84022647], one of eastern Oregon’s oldest papers. The Bedrock Democrat was first published on May 11, 1870 by former Confederate army officer Lewis Linn McArthur, and Oregon newspaper pioneer Milton H. Abbott. Abbott had previously founded the Portland Daily Oregon Herald [LCCN: sn85033164] and was partial owner of the Albany State Rights Democrat [LCCN: sn84022644]. After two years at the Bedrock Democrat, MacArthur retired, leaving Abbott as the sole proprietor.  Abbott worked with the Oregon City Enterprise [LCCN: sn94052321] in 1870, and later founded the Pendleton Eastern Oregon Tribune [LCCN: sn93051635] in 1873, followed by The Dalles Oregon Weekly Tribune [LCCN: sn84022685] in 1875. Abbott became editor of the La Grande Gazette [LCCN: sn93051649] in 1877 and edited The Dalles Oregon Democratic Journal [LCCN: unknown] from 1884 to 1885.

In 1872, Jesse M. Shepherd bought the Bedrock Democrat and published the paper for eight years with his son, Harry C. Shepherd. In 1873, R.B. Boyd & Co. established the Baker City Weekly Herald [LCCN: sn93051609], a Democratic competitor, but by 1875 the Herald became Republican and ceased publishing shortly after. In 1880, Shepherd sold the Democrat to the Bedrock Publishing Company, with Ira B. Bowen and J.T. Donnelly as editors. During that time, Abbott, one of the Democrat’s original founders, began working with another competitor, the Baker County Reveille [LCCN: sn93051607], a Democratic paper that eventually suspended in the late 1880s.

In 1887, Bowen & Small, of which Donnelly was a part, purchased the paper, changing the paper’s title to the Daily Morning Democrat [LCCN: sn96088185]. The name was changed to the Morning Democrat [LCCN: sn85042398] in 1892 and continued to print every morning except Monday. As of 1893 the paper had a steam Cottrell rotary press, and the plant had 12 employees and was worth about $10,000. Covering local, state, national and international news and politics, public opinions, and theatrical events, the newspaper asserted: “Everybody reads the Democrat.” The 1890s brought weekly circulation to 2500, with a daily circulation of 1200.

The Morning Democrat claimed to be “always in the lead” as “the distributing point for the mining districts of eastern Oregon.” A special illustrated souvenir mining edition published in 1900 reported on “Eastern Oregon’s gold fields – marvelous wealth in mining, farming, lumbering…stock and fruit raising…” Other issues from the early 1900s included reports on legislation related to the treatment of Native Americans, Japanese immigration, African American soldiers, and race issues in the U.S. Senate, as well as mining strikes and union news. The Democrat sometimes published non-news items such as the “Indian Legend of the John Day River.” Advertisements for women’s apparel were plentiful, but coverage of women’s issues was generally lacking, save for opinion columns such as one 1907 article titled “Women Oppose the Woman Suffrage Move.” By 1906, the paper’s motto became “Baker City First, Last, and all the Time.”

In 1919 Will H. Evans took partial ownership of the Democrat, and in 1929, Ernest L. Crockatt took over Bowen’s interest. Ralph H. Mitchell, previous editor of the Portland Oregonian [LCCN: sn83025138], took over for Crockatt, and gained full ownership of the paper. In 1929, the Democrat merged with the Evening Baker Herald [LCCN: sn96088188], forming the Democrat-Herald [LCCN: sn85000362], an evening newspaper under the leadership of editor Bernard Mainwaring and manager Lucien P. Arant. Evans remained as advertising manager. The paper was published every evening except Sunday until 1990.
