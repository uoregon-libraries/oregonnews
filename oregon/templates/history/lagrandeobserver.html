La Grande Observer
[LCCN: 2006260039]
La Grande, Union County, Oregon
1896 â€“ 1968

 

The town of La Grande was founded in northeastern Oregon in 1861 by Oregon Trail immigrants and was designated as the county seat of Union County, nestled between the Wallowa Mountains and the Blue Mountains in the Grande Ronde River valley. Nearby gold mines and rich valley agriculture drew more settlers to La Grande in the 1870s. A railroad was constructed through the area in 1884, bolstering the economy and leading to further expansion of the town.

George Hoskins Currey, previous editor of the Grant’s Pass Courier [LCCN: sn96088280] founded La Grande’s Eastern Oregon Observer [LCCN: 2006260032] in 1896 as a weekly paper in support of the Populist Party. Currey used a printing plant that was moved to La Grande from Baker City, and the Observer started with eight-column pages at 20x25 inches in size.

Fred B. Currey joined the business in 1897, and the brothers launched the Daily Morning Observer [LCCN: 2006260037] in addition to the weekly Observer, in support of the political campaign that year. When the Populist Party fused with the Democratic Party in 1898, the Observer claimed to be politically Independent, but soon revealed Republican leanings. The temporary daily publication was so popular that the La Grande Morning Observer [LCCN: 2006260038] was formally established on November 1, 1901, and in 1903, daily publication changed from mornings to evenings with a title change to the La Grande Evening Observer [LCCN: 2006260039].

The daily paper featured striking, bold headlines such as “Use Bombs to Kill Capitalists of Wall Street” (1908), and did not shy away from rhetoric of race and ethnicity, featuring headlines such as “Japs Assume the Aggressive” (1908), “Indian Lands Leased: Long winded affair requiring much red tape” (1904), “Congress May Oust Negroes from United States Army: worthy in battle but a menace in peace” (1906), and “Wealthy Mexican Arrested” (1906).

Crime, railroad and fire accidents, international relations, politics, and legal decisions often took the front page, while local social updates, news from other states, classified ads, and church service listings were surrounded by local advertisements within the paper. Early issues emphasized the importance of advances in industry, new homes and buildings, and a 1905 article asserted that active communities attract active people and growth: “That La Grande has grown to be one of the leading cities in the state is patent, and the same conditions that have caused its development in the past will cause future growth.” By 1916 the Evening Observer included a dedicated Editorial Page, loaded with how-to articles for the home and garden, farming advice, fashion news, and political cartoons.

The weekly publication was also still in full swing, with layout and content similar to the daily, and its name was changed from the Eastern Oregon Observer to the La Grande Weekly Observer [LCCN: 2006260033] in 1905, costing $1.00 for a year’s subscription. Unlike many historic newspapers in Oregon, the Observer provided sports coverage in nearly every issue, and the La Grande football team was pictured in the December 22, 1905 issue of the Weekly Observer.

Fred Currey left the paper in 1907, leaving George in full control once again, and a machine composition No. 5 Mergenthaler was also brought in to the plant that year. In 1910, Bruce Dennis, editor of the Baker City Herald [LCCN: sn96088192], came to La Grande and bought the Observer. One year later, the Observer purchased the La Grande Morning Star [LCCN: 2006260042], which was founded in 1907 by Molly K. Proebstel. In 1914, Dennis sold the Observer to Clark Leiter, previous editor of the Portland Morning Oregonian [LCCN: sn83025138], in partnership with Mrs. Leiter and Don Meyers. Leiter installed the first web press in La Grande and added a metropolitan element to the paper. In 1918, Dennis resumed ownership of the Observer titles, and Leiter went on to edit the Portland Telegram [LCCN: sn86063804], later serving as a professor of journalism at the University of Illinois.

The La Grande Weekly Observer added the La Grande Star to the title [LCCN: 2006260035], changing the weekly’s name to The Observer-Star [LCCN: 2006260036] in 1921. In 1925, the Observer was sold to Frank B. Appleby and Harve Mathews, while Dennis left La Grande and purchased Klamath Falls’ The Evening Herald [LCCN: sn99063812].

P.R. Finlay bought the Observer in 1930, and his son, Harold M. Finlay, took over from 1932 to 1938 following his father’s death. In 1959, the daily publication changed names from the La Grande Evening Observer to the La Grande Observer [LCCN: 2006260040], published every day except Sunday. In 1968 the name was shortened to simply The Observer [LCCN: 2006260041], which is still in publication today.
